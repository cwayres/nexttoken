How Autoregressive Decoding Works

▼

Large language models are autoregressive: they generate text one token at a time. At each step the model's transformer layers produce a logit vector over its entire vocabulary. A softmax converts these logits into a probability distribution, and a sampling strategy (controlled by temperature) selects the next token. That token is appended to the context and the process repeats — this is the autoregressive loop. What did the model almost say?

Before the model writes the very first token of its response, it scores every word-piece in its vocabulary and ranks them by confidence. A high probability on one token means the model was confident; similar probabilities across several means it was torn between options.

Candidate token Confidence LogProb ⓘLogProb = ln(probability). A value of 0 means 100% confidence. More negative = less likely.

Examples: −0.10 ≈ 90%, −1.0 ≈ 37%, −3.0 ≈ 5%
Convert: probability = e^logprob

How confident was the model?

Each bar shows how much of the model's confidence went to that candidate. A single long bar means near-certainty. Multiple similar bars mean the model sees several plausible next words.
